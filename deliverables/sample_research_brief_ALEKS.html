Grade 6 students who spent more time on ALEKS tended to score higher on the mid-year FastBridge aMath assessment — an effect size of +0.20 (partial correlation, 95% CI: 0.12–0.28), which is statistically significant and consistent with ESSA Level III Promising Evidence. This positive relationship held across most student subgroups, with the strongest associations among students in the highest prior-performance tier (ES +0.32) and White students (ES +0.25). However, only 27% of students met the recommended dosage of 665 minutes (≈30 min/week) over the 25-week window — a low-usage, high-impact pattern that suggests expanding access to the recommended dosage could meaningfully amplify outcomes across the student population.

Low Usage, High Impact — A Key Equity Opportunity
Despite 97% of students logging into ALEKS at least once, only 27% met the recommended dosage of 665 minutes. Yet the product shows a statistically significant positive relationship with math outcomes at this partial usage level. This pattern suggests that the product has real instructional value — and that increasing the share of students reaching dosage recommendations could substantially expand benefits, particularly for students currently using the platform below threshold. Special Education students met the dosage goal at less than half the rate of General Education students (14% vs. 30%), a gap that warrants focused attention.

Study Limitations
  • Correlational design — cannot establish that ALEKS caused the score gains; other factors may explain the relationship
  • Only Grade 6 analyzed — insufficient data for other grade levels, limiting generalizability
  • Low dosage compliance (27%) means most effect size estimates reflect below-recommended usage; outcomes at full dosage may differ
  • Several ethnicity subgroups (Asian, Native Hawaiian/Pacific Islander, American Indian/Alaska Native) had n < 30 and could not generate stable effect size estimates
  • Single school (Southeast Polk Middle School) — results reflect one local context
📌 Recommended Next Steps ▼

Discuss results with the district team (see Appendix C of the full report). Consider the following actions:

  • Review barriers to dosage compliance, particularly for Special Education students (14% met goal vs. 30% General Ed)
  • Implement usage monitoring and nudge systems to support students approaching — but not yet meeting — the 665-minute threshold
  • Consider additional EOY RCEs using updated 2025-2026 usage and outcomes data to track trajectory
  • Investigate why Hispanic/Latino and American Indian/Alaska Native students logged fewer average minutes and whether scheduling or access factors are contributing
